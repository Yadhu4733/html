Edgar Allan Poe (1809-1849) was an American writer, poet, and literary critic who is best known for his macabre and Gothic tales and poems. He is considered one of the most influential figures in American literature and a pioneer of the short story genre. Poe's works often explore themes of death, madness, and the dark side of human nature. Born on January 19, 1809, in Boston, Massachusetts, Poe had a tumultuous life marked by personal tragedy and professional struggles. His father abandoned the family early on, and his mother died when he was just three years old. He was taken in by John and Frances Allan, wealthy tobacco merchants from Richmond, Virginia, who provided him with a good education but also a strict upbringing.

Poe attended the University of Virginia but had to leave due to financial difficulties. He joined the Army and published his first collection of poems, "Tamerlane and Other Poems," anonymously in 1827. In 1836, Poe married his cousin, Virginia Clemm, who was only 13 years old at the time. Poe's literary career began to gain recognition in the 1840s when he published some of his most famous works, including "The Fall of the House of Usher," "The Tell-Tale Heart," and "The Raven." His writing style was characterized by its dark and mysterious atmosphere, vivid imagery, and intricate plots.
